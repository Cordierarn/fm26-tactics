4. Tactical Identity: Who Are You on The
Touchline?
Next question: What do your teams actually play like? Not what you think is meta, not what your favourite YouTuber uploaded this week - but you. Imagine the camera pans to the touchline in real life. The commentator says: "You know exactly what you get from a [Your Name] side." What is he describing? Here are a few classic identities. You might be one, or a blend of two.
· The Pressing Evangelist You live for counter-pressing, traps in wide areas, centre-backs on the halfway line. You accept the risk of balls over the top because you want to suffocate the opposition.
· The Control Freak You want ninety minutes of structure and calm. Slow build up, calculated risk, players always offering passing lanes. You would rather win 1-0 with 70 percent possession, than 4-3 as a result of chaos.
· The Counter Assassin Compact block, disciplined shape, then lightning-fast transitions. You love seeing the opposition full-backs caught high and your winger sprinting into the space left behind.
· The Hybrid Pragmatist You mix it. Press high against weaker sides, sit off against giants, switch shapes comfortably. You are defined by adaptability, not by one idea.
Pick the one that matches your personality. Do not lie. If you hate slow football, do not pretend to be a possession-oriented coach. You will get bored and abandon the save. In later chapters, we will translate this identity into exact roles and instructions. For now, all we need is your headline: "My FM26 tactical identity: __________." Write it. That is your touchline badge.

5. Understanding the FM26 Journey: Seasons, Cycles, And Turning Points
FM saves are not one long blur, even if they feel like it at 2 a.m. They are made of cycles. Think in blocks of three seasons:
· Season 1 ­ Discovery and survival. Learn the squad, stabilise results, move on obvious deadwood, set the tone.
· Season 2 ­ Consolidation. Core of the team is now yours, tactics feel natural, finances start to reflect your decisions.
· Season 3 ­ Statement. Title challenge, deep Cup runs, or a clear identity that everyone recognises.
Then the cycle restarts. New leaders emerge, old heroes age, expectations rise. Within each cycle, there are turning points.
· The first, big derby win, which buys you time. · The first dressing room revolt, which exposes weaknesses in your authority. · The big sale that funds your rebuild, or the big signing that upsets the wage
structure. Good managers anticipate these moments. They do not panic when they arrive because they already know what they stand for. In this guide, we will often ask "Where are you in the cycle?" so keep this in your head. It will stop you making desperate decisions just because you lost three on the bounce in October.

6. Risk Profile: Are You a Gambler or a Safe Pair of Hands?
Every decision in FM sits somewhere on a risk line. · Selling your top scorer to fund a flurry of youngsters. · Promising a player "top half finish" when the media predicts relegation. · Pushing your defensive line higher than you really trust.
There is no correct level of risk. The wrong move is pretending you are one thing when you are actually another. Ask yourself a few quick questions:
1. When a big club bids for your star player, do you usually say yes or fight to keep him? 2. When you see a raw 19-year-old with great potential, but mediocre current ability,
do you feel excited or concerned? 3. With the scores tied level, do you regularly push for the win or take the point? If you often chase upside, you are naturally more of a gambler. If you usually protect what you have, you are more of a safe pair of hands. Neither is wrong. But each demands a different approach. · Gamblers must become disciplined with finances and promises, because one reckless
decision can break a save. · Safe managers must push themselves to take calculated shots when opportunity
knocks, or they will stagnate. Write your risk type next to your tactical identity. For example: "Aggressive presser, medium risk" or "Control style, low risk." Now we are really starting to see who you are.

7. Non-Negotiables: Rules You Will Not Break in This Save
Every great dressing room has rules on the wall. Some are obvious: `Be on Time, Work Hard'. The powerful ones are personal. Your FM26 save should have the same. These are your non-negotiables. Once you write them, you keep them. No excuses at 1 a.m. when the classic transfer deadline panic sets in. Here are examples you can steal or adapt:
· "No players aged over 30 on long-term, high-wage deals." · "Always keep at least one academy graduate in the starting eleven when possible." · "Never sign from direct rivals, unless it fits the story of the save." · "No rage-quitting, every bad run must be solved inside the game." · "Do not change tactics more than twice in a season without a clear reason." Write three to five. Put them somewhere visible. You will be amazed how much stronger your save feels when you stop bending your own rules.

8. Translating Real Life Football Values into FM26 Decisions
Everyone who loves FM loves real football first. You probably already have managers you admire, teams you still watch highlights of, and football ideas that make you smile. We are going to use them. 9. Your First Big Choice: Picking the Right FM26
Starting Club
Now, at last, we talk about clubs. Every year people ask, "Who should I manage?" as if there is one magic answer. There is not. There is only "Who fits your Manager DNA best?" Use your notes from this chapter and test clubs against them. Ask for each option:
· Does this club suit my preferred playing style? A pressing identity might struggle at first in a very slow, ageing squad, which is fine if you enjoy a rebuild but painful if you want instant results.
· Does the financial situation match my risk profile? If you are a natural gambler, a club on the edge of administration might be emotional suicide. If you are cautious, a `sugar daddy' club could feel dull.
· Does the club's culture align with my manifesto? Youth-focused managers will be happier at clubs with good facilities and a history of developing players.
· Does the league's structure fit the story I want to tell? Promotion battles feel different in England than in leagues with play-offs or confusing, foreign registration rules.
Write down three candidates: 1. One comfort pick that feels like a perfect match. 2. One slightly harder pick that will challenge you. 3. One wildcard that you are secretly drawn to.

You are no longer "some bloke playing FM26." You are a manager with a vision, a style, a risk profile, and a set of rules. FM26 has never needed that more. 8. Budget, Wages, and Sell-On Clauses: Playing the Long Transfer Game
Every signing you make in FM26 has two price tags: · The fee you pay · The effect it has on your Wage and Clause structure
Long-term stability comes from respecting both. Some principles to live by:
· Decide a loose wage band for each squad status. Key Players, First Teamers, Rotational, Backups, Hot Prospects. Try not to let anyone punch too far above their band unless they are obviously special (Ballon d'Or-level talent, for example).
· Use instalments smartly. Paying 50 percent now and 50 percent over three years can turn a risky move into a manageable one, as long as you are tracking future commitments.
· Do not be afraid of sell-on clauses in both directions. Keeping a slice of a player you sell can fund your future rebuilds if he explodes. Adding sell-on clauses instead of higher fees can make deals happen that you could not otherwise afford.
Most importantly, link financial decisions back to your tactic. If a player does not fit your IP and OOP roles, he is not a "bargain". He is dead money. 5. Final Words from Roy
If you remember nothing else from this book, remember this. Football Manager 26 is not here to give you a perfect save. It is here to give you a beautifully messy sandbox where:
· tactics are clever enough to reward real thinking, · people are moody enough to feel alive, · boards and fans are irrational enough to keep you honest, · the interface is annoying enough to force you to build your own shortcuts, · and stories are long enough to stay with you when the laptop is shut. Your job is not to "solve" the game. Your job is to: · pick a way you want to win, · build a club that can play that way, · keep the people inside it just about on your side, · spend money like it is real, · and give yourself time to see the payoff. Do that, and it does not matter if you ever post screenshots or write threads.
